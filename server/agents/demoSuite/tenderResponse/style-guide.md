## 1. Firm Identity Statement

Curam Engineering is a Queensland-based specialist contractor with 22 years of continuous operation in marine and coastal infrastructure. We are not a generalist civil contractor who occasionally works near water — our full business is marine. Every project in our reference library was delivered in a tidal, coastal, or port environment. This distinction should be evident in every paragraph of every response we write.

## 2. Non-Negotiable Writing Rules

### 2.1 Active Voice, Specific Claims
**Wrong:** *"It is anticipated that a methodology will be developed..."*
**Right:** *"Our team will deploy a three-stage pile assessment programme using UPV testing and targeted core sampling."*

Every sentence should name an actor, a method, and a measurable outcome where possible.

### 2.2 Metric-First Outcomes
Lead with the number. Don't bury performance evidence in a paragraph of context.
**Wrong:** *"The Port Hedland project was well-received and delivered safely."*
**Right:** *"Port Hedland Marine Hub (2023, $12.4M, C5-M): ICCP commissioned on schedule; 50-year design life verified; LTIFR 0.0 across 14 months of construction."*

### 2.3 Australian Standards as Floor, Not Ceiling
Never frame compliance with an Australian Standard as an achievement. Frame it as a baseline, then state what the firm does beyond it.
**Wrong:** *"We will comply with AS 2832.1 for cathodic protection design."*
**Right:** *"Our ICCP designs are verified against AS 2832.1 and independently tested at commissioning — a practice we have applied on every cathodic protection project since 2019."*

### 2.4 Safety Language
Do not use passive or aspirational safety language in tender responses.
**Never use:** *"Safety is our highest priority."* (Every firm says this; it means nothing.)
**Use instead:** Specific LTIFR data, near-miss reporting rates, specific safe work method statements referenced by name, and named personnel accountable for WHS outcomes.

### 2.5 Queensland Orientation
Default to Queensland references and regulatory context unless the project is interstate. Reference QLD legislation (Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (Qld)), QLD-specific standards (Port of Brisbane Standard Technical Specifications 2024), and QLD entities (Workplace Health and Safety Queensland, Department of Environment and Science).

## 3. Sentence and Paragraph Style

| Rule | Detail |
|---|---|
| Sentence length | Max 25 words per sentence in technical sections. Evaluators skim. |
| Paragraph length | Max 5 sentences per paragraph. Break into sub-sections generously. |
| Jargon | Use standard Australian engineering terminology. Do not use US terminology (e.g., say "programme" not "schedule", "tender" not "bid", "subcontractor" not "vendor"). |
| Acronyms | Define on first use in every volume. Do not assume the evaluator read another volume. |
| Tense | Use future tense for methodology ("We will..."), present tense for capability ("Curam holds..."), and past tense for evidence ("The Port Hedland project achieved..."). |

## 5. Prohibited Claims — Do Not Use Without Explicit Sign-Off

| Prohibited Claim | Reason |
|---|---|
| Any claim of "award-winning" performance | Not verified in evidence library |
| Reference to any project not in the Project Experience Library | Unverified; potential NDA exposure |
| Claiming ISO 45001 is "current" without noting renewal | CRT-003 is in renewal; use "renewal in progress" language |
| Stating specific design life figures not validated in project record | Design life claims require RPEQ sign-off |
| Use of "guaranteed" in relation to outcomes | Creates contractual exposure |
| Naming client personnel as referees without confirming they are still in role | Contact details must be verified before submission |

---

## 6. Evidence Citation Format

Every factual claim in a technical response must be traceable. Use the following inline citation format:

> *"Curam designed and commissioned an ICCP system for the Mackay Harbour Berth 6 & 7 retrofit project in 2024 [REF-005], achieving AS 2832.1 compliance verified by independent third-party testing."*

For personnel claims:

> *"The project will be led by James Whitfield (PER-001, RPEQ No. 18274), who has 28 years of marine infrastructure experience across 12 port projects in WA, QLD, and NT."*

## 7. Volume-Specific Tone Guidance

| Volume | Tone |
|---|---|
| Volume 1 – Tender Response Form | Formal, declarative. Match the format of the form exactly. No embellishment. |
| Volume 2 – Commercial Schedule | Neutral, precise. No marketing language. Numbers speak. |
| Volume 3 – Technical Methodology | Confident and specific. Use sub-headings, methodology steps, named standards, and named personnel. This is where we win or lose. |
| Volume 4 – Corporate Compliance | Clinical and factual. Certificate numbers, dates, regulatory references. No narrative. |

---

## 8. Evaluator Psychology Notes

Port authority evaluators are typically experienced engineers or project managers. They will:
- Skip paragraphs that don't open with a claim or a number
- Notice immediately if C5-M experience is claimed but not evidenced
- Check whether the RPEQ number is real and current
- Distrust any WHS section that doesn't cite actual incident statistics

Write for a sceptical expert, not a receptive generalist.
